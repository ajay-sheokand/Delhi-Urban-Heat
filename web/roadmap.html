The Approach

The Problem

Landsat 8 revisits Delhi roughly every 16 days, but usable land-surface-temperature scenes require the sky to actually be clear — cloud cover, especially during monsoon months, silently removes a large share of that already-sparse schedule (see the real gap data above). Any product built purely on optical imagery inherits these blind spots by construction, exactly like the map layers earlier on this site.

Why SAR

Synthetic Aperture Radar (e.g. Sentinel-1) images through cloud cover and at night, because it's an active sensor operating in the microwave range rather than reflected sunlight or thermal emission. It doesn't directly measure the same physical quantities as optical/thermal sensors, but its backscatter is correlated with surface structure, moisture, and vegetation — enough signal, in principle, to learn a mapping back to optical-equivalent bands.

Our Approach: SAR→Optical Reconstruction

We're training a Graph Neural Network to reconstruct optical-band imagery (and by extension, LST/NDVI-relevant signal) directly from SAR input, for the specific dates and locations where cloud cover would otherwise leave a gap in the record — using paired SAR/optical observations from clear-sky dates as ground truth during training.

Architecture details, training data, and validation results go here once training completes and there's a score to report.

What This Unlocks

If it works, every gap in the chart above becomes a filled data point instead of a missing one — turning this from a periodic satellite snapshot into a genuinely continuous heat-monitoring signal. That's the differentiator: not another heat dashboard, but the ability to see through the exact condition (clouds) that breaks every optical-only competitor.
